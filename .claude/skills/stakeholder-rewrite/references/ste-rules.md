# ASD-STE100 Issue 9 — rule map

Paraphrased working map, verified against the official Issue 9 PDF (2025-01-15). The standard's own text is not reproduced here: ASD's copyright notice prohibits reproduction or publication in whole or in part without written authority from an ASD officer, and prohibits redistribution through other websites or portals. Read the official copy for rule text and examples; use this map to know where to look.

Part 1 is 53 writing rules in 9 sections, plus 8 General Recommendations. Part 2 is the dictionary.

## Section 1 — Words (1.1 to 1.14)

- **1.1** Permitted words are: approved dictionary words, technical nouns, technical verbs.
- **1.2** Use an approved word only as its specified part of speech. *Test* is an approved noun but not a verb.
- **1.3** Use approved words only with their approved meaning. *Follow* means come after — not obey.
- **1.4** Only approved forms of verbs and adjectives.
- **1.5** Technical nouns — **22 categories**. Directly useful ones outside aerospace: **19** computer science and ICT (includes *authentication*, *database*, *large language model*, *cybersecurity*, *token*), **21** law and regulations (*compliance*, *contract*, *scope*, *waiver*, *jurisdiction*), **15** official documents and standards, **11** professional roles and organizations, **5** facilities and logistics.
- **1.6** Non-approved words are usable only as, or within, a technical noun.
- **1.7** Do not use technical nouns as verbs. "Apply oil to the surfaces", not "Oil the surfaces."
- **1.8** Use the technical noun your organisation already approves.
- **1.9** If you must coin one, keep it short (max three words) and plain.
- **1.10** No regional, slang, or jargon technical nouns.
- **1.11** Never two names for one thing. The single most valuable rule for stakeholder writing.
- **1.12** Technical verbs — 4 categories: manufacturing processes; computer processes and applications; subject-field instructions (engineering, medical, civil/military, navigation, automotive/rail, energy); **law and regulations** (*comply with*, *conform to*, *enforce*, *notify*, *supersede*, *waive*).
- **1.13** Do not use technical verbs as nouns.
- **1.14** American English spelling **unless other official directives apply**. See the note on British English below.

## Section 2 — Multi-word nouns (2.1, 2.2)

Retitled in Issue 9 from "noun clusters" to **multi-word nouns**.

- **2.1** Maximum three words. Hyphenated compounds count as one word.
- **2.2** Longer approved technical nouns: write in full on first use, then either give a defined short form or hyphenate the words that act as one unit.

Old rule 2.3 moved to 4.5 in Issue 9.

## Section 3 — Verbs (3.1 to 3.7)

- **3.1** Only the verb forms the dictionary gives.
- **3.2** Permitted forms and tenses: infinitive, imperative, **simple present, simple past, simple future**, past participle as adjective. Not permitted: present perfect, past perfect, progressives, and other complex constructions.
- **3.3** Past participle as adjective — before a noun, or after *be*, *become*, *stay*. This is not passive voice.
- **3.4** No auxiliary verbs building complex constructions.
- **3.5** The *-ing* form only as a technical noun or as a modifier inside one. Approved *-ing* words are few: *lighting*, *opening*, *routing*, *servicing*, *mating*, *missing*, *remaining*, *something*, *during*.
- **3.6** Active voice. Passive is permitted **only in descriptive writing and only when the agent is genuinely unknown** — the standard's own test is whether "by whom or by what?" has an answer. If it does, rewrite active.
- **3.7** Describe an action with a verb, not a nominalisation.

## Section 4 — Sentences (4.1 to 4.5)

- **4.1** One topic per sentence.
- **4.2** Do not omit words for brevity.
- **4.3** Vertical lists, including how they interact with sentence length.
- **4.4** Connecting words and phrases.
- **4.5** Articles and demonstrative adjectives (moved here from 2.3 in Issue 9).

Sentence-length limits sit across 4.1 to 4.3, 5.1, 6.3, and 8.4 to 8.7 — the commonly cited figures are 20 words for procedural and 25 for descriptive. Confirm against the official copy before quoting them in anything auditable; the word-count rules in 8.6 changed in Issue 9.

## Section 5 — Procedural writing (5.1 to 5.5)

Imperative, one action per step, conditions stated before the action, and rule **5.5** on notes.

## Section 6 — Descriptive writing (6.1 to 6.6)

Topic per paragraph (6.4, 6.5), paragraph length (6.6), key words and phrases (6.2), sentence length (6.3).

## Section 7 — Safety instructions (7.1 to 7.3)

Warnings and cautions precede the step they govern. Aligned to ANSI Z535 and ISO 3864.

## Section 8 — Punctuation and word count (8.1 to 8.7)

8.1 to 8.3 punctuation; 8.4 to 8.7 word count. **8.6** covers acronyms, initialisms, headings, titles, quoted text, units, and — new in Issue 9 — proper nouns of individuals, groups, organizations, and geopolitical entities.

## Section 9 — Writing practices (9.1 to 9.4) and General Recommendations

- **9.1** Different sentence constructions when word-for-word replacement fails.
- **9.2** Approved words and meanings in practice.
- **9.4** Consistent style.

General Recommendations, two of them new in Issue 9 and both relevant here:

- GR-1 *that* — GR-2 *with* — GR-3 pronouns — GR-4 *this* — GR-5 false friends — GR-6 Latin abbreviations
- **GR-7 Inclusive language** (new in Issue 9; the standard cites the EIGE gender-sensitive communication toolkit and the UN disability-inclusive communications guidelines)
- **GR-8 Possessive form** (new in Issue 9)

## Spelling: the British English conflict

Rule 1.14 mandates American English, based on Merriam-Webster — but explicitly yields to "other technical publication specifications, style guides, contracts, or other official directives."

For a UK organisation writing to UK stakeholders, American spelling would be wrong and distracting. Use the escape clause: treat the organisation's house style as the applicable official directive, write British English, and record that as a deliberate deviation from 1.14 rather than an oversight. If a document is going to a body that expects strict STE, raise it before drafting rather than after.

## What the standard says about its own limits

Worth knowing, because it settles arguments:

- STE controls how content is expressed. It does **not** regulate formatting, units of measurement, or abbreviations — those belong to the house style guide.
- STE is not meant to be used alone. It expects to sit alongside a style guide and publication specification.
- STE is not an English course, and writing it correctly requires high fluency.
- Improving translation quality is an explicit design goal, and Issue 9 names neural machine translation and large language models as beneficiaries.
